Mountains

A mountain is a large, elevated natural landform that rises significantly above the surrounding terrain, often with steep slopes and a peak. Mountains are formed by tectonic forces, such as the collision of Earth's crust, or by volcanic activity. They are distinct from hills by their greater steepness and elevation, generally considered higher than 300 meters. Mountains are home to unique ecosystems and wildlife adapted to cold, high-altitude environments, and they play a crucial role in influencing regional climates and serving as sources for many rivers.

Sky

The sky is the space above the Earth where the sun, moon, stars, and clouds are seen, appearing as a blue dome by day due to light scattering and a dark expanse of stars by night. It can also refer to the atmosphere, especially its lower layers when discussing weather, or the celestial sphere, an abstract dome on which celestial bodies seem to move. Descriptions vary based on time of day and conditions, ranging from sunny and clear to cloudy and grey, or even red and orange during sunrise and sunset. Components and Appearance Daytime: On a clear day, the sky appears blue because air molecules scatter shorter blue wavelengths of sunlight more effectively than longer, redder wavelengths. Nighttime: The night sky is a dark expanse, often referred to as the night sky, where the moon and stars are visible. Celestial Sphere: In astronomy, the sky is also known as the celestial sphere, a conceptual sphere on which the sun, moon, planets, and stars appear to be located. Clouds: Clouds are a visible phenomenon in the sky, with swift movement across the expanse being a common sight.

Ocean

The ocean is a huge body of saltwater that covers about 71 percent of Earth's surface. The planet has one global ocean, though oceanographers and the countries of the world have traditionally divided it into five distinct regions: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, and Arctic oceans
